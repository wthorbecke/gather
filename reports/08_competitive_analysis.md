## Competitor Overview

### ADHD-Specific Apps

**Tiimo** - The current market leader for ADHD planning. Won iPhone App of the Year 2025. Visual daily planner with AI task breakdown, focus timers, mood check-ins, and 3,000+ customization options. Pricing: ~$42-54/year. iOS only (no Android as of 2026).

**Llama Life** - Time-boxed task lists with countdown timers, designed by someone with ADHD for ADHD. Shows total list time and estimated finish time to combat time blindness. Confetti on completion. No free tier after 7-day trial.

**Structured** - Visual timeline planner for daily scheduling. Simple, GTD-inspired, with Pomodoro timer and widgets. Free tier is limited; calendar sync requires Pro. iCloud sync issues reported. Android/web lag behind iOS.

**Routinery** - Routine builder with time-boxed scheduling and visual/audio signals for transitions. Good for morning/evening routines. Affordable at ~$27/year. More routine-focused than task-focused.

**Focusmate** - Virtual body doubling platform pairing users with accountability partners via video. 25/50/75-minute sessions. Free tier: 3 sessions/week. Claims 161% productivity increase for neurodivergent users. Solves accountability, not task breakdown.

### Mainstream Task Managers

**Todoist** - The standard. Clean, reliable, cross-platform. AI assistant in Pro plan suggests priorities and schedules. Free tier generous. Pro: $4-5/month. Trusted brand, massive user base. No ADHD-specific features.

**Things 3** - Beautiful Apple-only task manager. GTD methodology. One-time purchase (~$80 for all devices). No AI, no collaboration, no web version. Premium feel, premium constraints.

**TickTick** - Feature-rich with Pomodoro timer, habit tracker, calendar view. Very affordable (~$36/year). No AI features. Good for power users who want everything in one app.

### AI-Powered Productivity

**Motion** - Aggressive AI auto-scheduling. Creates your entire day from tasks/deadlines. $29/month minimum. Steep learning curve (1-2 weeks). Best for structured knowledge workers. Not designed for ADHD.

**Reclaim.ai** - AI calendar scheduling for work-life balance. Auto-schedules habits, focus time, breaks. Free tier available. $8-18/month for paid. Google Calendar & Outlook integration. Task syncing from other apps.

**Sunsama** - Mindful daily planner with integrations. Pull tasks from Jira/Trello/Asana. Daily shutdown ritual. $16-20/month. Mobile experience poor. Not task breakdown, just task aggregation.

## True Differentiators

What Gather does that NO ONE else does:

### 1. **AI Research + Task Breakdown Combined**
Gather's AI doesn't just break tasks down—it researches them first. When you say "File my taxes," it searches the web for California-specific FTB links, finds deadlines, and includes direct action URLs. No other app does this.

**Why it matters:** ADHD brains get stuck at "I don't know where to start." Gather removes that wall completely.

### 2. **Pre-filled Action URLs and Direct Links**
Steps include `tel:` links with actual phone numbers, payment page URLs, case numbers—everything needed to execute, not just understand.

**Why it matters:** Reduces friction from "I know what to do" to "I'm doing it" to nearly zero.

### 3. **AI Clarifying Questions Flow**
Before generating steps, Gather asks targeted questions (state for taxes, case number for tickets). This context makes the breakdown actually useful.

**Why it matters:** Generic steps are useless. Personalized steps with your actual data are actionable.

### 4. **"I'm Stuck" Chat Interface**
When a step feels impossible, users can get step-specific AI help. Not a general chatbot—context-aware assistance on exactly what they're struggling with.

**Why it matters:** Addresses the ADHD "wall" in real-time, not just at task creation.

### 5. **Rich Step Metadata**
Each step includes: title, summary, detailed explanation, time estimate, alternatives, examples, checklists, source links, and action URLs. No other task app provides this depth.

**Why it matters:** Users never wonder "but how?" Everything they need is already there.

## Positioning Analysis

**Gather is currently positioned as:**
An AI-powered task manager for people with ADHD that researches and breaks down overwhelming tasks, then stays available to help when users get stuck.

**Gather should be positioned as:**
**"The executive function you wish you had."** Not a todo app. Not a planner. An AI that does the cognitive work of breaking down scary tasks, does the research you'd procrastinate on, and hands you one small step at a time.

**The competitive set is really:**
1. **Direct:** Tiimo, Llama Life (ADHD task apps with AI)
2. **Adjacent:** Goblin Tools (free AI task breakdown, no app)
3. **Aspirational:** A human executive function coach

**Where Gather should NOT compete:**
- Against Todoist/Things 3 (general task management)
- Against Motion/Reclaim (calendar AI)
- Against Structured (visual planning)

## Brutal Assessment

**Why would anyone choose Gather over Todoist/Tiimo/Motion?**

Over Todoist: Because Todoist doesn't help you figure out HOW to do the task. It just holds the task. If you write "Deal with medical bills" in Todoist, it sits there judging you. In Gather, AI researches your state's dispute process, finds the phone numbers, and gives you a script.

Over Tiimo: This is harder. Tiimo has brand recognition (App Store Award), visual polish, and a mobile app. Gather's advantage is depth—richer research, actual links, ongoing chat support. But most users won't discover that depth before bouncing.

Over Motion: Different products. Motion is for people who want less control. Gather is for people who want more help. Not really competing.

**The honest answer:** Gather has a real differentiator (AI research + action URLs + ongoing help), but it's competing against an App of the Year winner with a web-only product and no brand recognition. The feature advantage is real; the distribution disadvantage is severe.

## The Moat Question

What's defensible? What stops a well-funded competitor from copying the good parts?

- [ ] **Proprietary technology** - AI task breakdown is replicable. Web search + Claude is not unique.
- [ ] **Network effects** - None. Individual productivity app.
- [ ] **Data advantage** - Potentially. Task completion patterns + context memory could compound.
- [ ] **Brand/community** - Not yet. No App Store presence, no community.
- [x] **None of the above** - Currently.

**Honest assessment:** The moat is weak. Tiimo could add web search to their AI breakdown tomorrow. The advantage is execution speed and ADHD-specific philosophy baked into every design decision—but philosophy isn't a moat.

**Potential moats to build:**
1. **Context memory that compounds** - The more you use Gather, the more it knows about your life (member IDs, preferred approaches, energy patterns). This becomes switching cost.
2. **Email/calendar integration depth** - Proactively surfacing tasks from email before user even asks.
3. **Community around ADHD authenticity** - If the voice/brand resonates deeply with ADHD users, loyalty follows.

## Strategic Recommendations

Not features to add—positioning and focus recommendations:

### 1. **Own "AI does the research" messaging**
Every competitor says "AI breakdown." Gather should say "AI researches your specific situation and gives you real links, real phone numbers, real scripts." Make the depth undeniable.

### 2. **Get on the App Store immediately**
Web-only is a death sentence for consumer productivity apps. Tiimo's App Store Award didn't come from a great product alone—it came from being discoverable. PWA is not enough.

### 3. **Target the "ADHD tax" pain point aggressively**
Position around money: "Gather pays for itself in late fees you'll never pay again." This is concrete, quantifiable, and different from "feel organized."

### 4. **Create a free tier with viral loop**
Goblin Tools went viral because it's free and instantly useful. Consider: free AI breakdown for 3 tasks/week, with shareable "here's my step-by-step plan" link.

### 5. **Don't chase the visual timeline feature**
Tiimo and Structured own visual. Motion owns auto-scheduling. Gather should own "AI that researches for you." Adding a timeline makes you worse at everything; deepening AI research makes you best at one thing.

## The Existential Question

**Should this app exist? Is there a gap in the market it fills?**

**Yes, but narrowly.**

The gap is real: existing ADHD apps break tasks into steps, but those steps are still generic. "Research payment options" is not actionable. "Go to ca.gov/dmv, enter case number 12345, pay $301" is actionable.

Gather fills the gap between "I know what I should do" and "I'm actually doing it" better than any competitor. The AI research + pre-filled actions + ongoing chat support is genuinely differentiated.

**However:**
- The gap is narrow enough that Tiimo could close it with one product update
- The distribution disadvantage (web-only, no brand) is severe
- The price sensitivity of ADHD users (often struggling financially) limits monetization

**Recommendation:** This app deserves to exist, but needs to move fast on mobile + distribution. The feature differentiation is real but perishable.
